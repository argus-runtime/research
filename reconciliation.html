§1

What is reconciliation?

The one-sentence version, then the picture.

Reconciliation is the act of comparing two independent records of the same money and making sure they agree — and when they don’t, finding out why and fixing it.

In plain English

Imagine you and a friend both keep a list of a shared trip’s expenses. At the end you sit down and check: “I have you paying ₹500 for lunch — do you?” Most lines match. A few don’t: you forgot one, they typo’d another, one payment hasn’t cleared yet. You go line by line until both lists tell the same story. That’s reconciliation. Now do it with thousands of transactions, real money, an auditor watching, and a deadline — that’s a finance team’s month.

In a company there are always at least two records of the same event. Example: your company’s own books (the “general ledger”) say you have ₹10,00,000 in the bank. The bank’s statement says ₹9,90,000. Both claim to be the truth. Reconciliation is how you find the ₹10,000 gap, explain it (a cheque that hasn’t cleared, a bank fee nobody recorded, a fraud, a typo), and get the two numbers to tie out.

Our books (ledger)

INV-1042  ·  +12,000 ✓
Salary run  ·  −4,80,000 ✓
Refund #88  ·  −1,500 ✓
— (no record) —
⇄MATCH

Bank statement

INV-1042  ·  +12,000 ✓
Salary run  ·  −4,80,000 ✓
Refund #88  ·  −1,500 ✓
Bank fee  ·  −350 ⚠

Three lines match cleanly. The bank fee exists on the bank side but was never recorded in the books — that’s an exception, and chasing it down is the actual job.

§2

Why do companies do it?

It’s not busywork — it’s the safety check on the numbers the whole business runs on.

01 Catch errors

Typos, double entries, wrong amounts, missed transactions. If the books are wrong, every decision built on them is wrong.

02 Detect fraud & leakage

Money leaving that shouldn’t, duplicate payments, skimming. Comparing independent records is one of the oldest fraud controls that exists.

03 Explain timing gaps

A payment you sent hasn’t cleared the bank yet. Nothing is wrong — but you must know that’s the reason the two numbers differ.

04 Trustworthy financials

Investors, lenders and leadership act on the reported numbers. Reconciliation is what lets finance sign off that they’re real.

05 Pass the audit & law

Auditors and regulations (e.g. SOX) effectively require reconciled accounts. No reconciliation, no clean audit.

06 Know your real cash

“How much money do we actually have right now?” is unanswerable if your books and your bank disagree.

The mental model

Reconciliation is to a company’s finances what testing is to your code. You’re not adding a feature — you’re proving the thing that’s already there is actually correct before anyone relies on it.

§3

The main types (so the jargon stops scaring you)

All of them are the same core move — “compare two records” — just different pairs of records.

bank Bank reconciliation

Company books vs. the bank statement. The classic, most common one.

“Our ledger says X, the bank says Y — why?”

card Credit-card / expense

Ledger entries vs. the card processor’s statement.

Matching every swipe to a recorded expense.

AP Vendor / payables

What you think you owe suppliers vs. what the supplier’s statement says.

“Their invoice says we owe ₹2L; our books say ₹1.8L.”

AR Customer / receivables

Invoices you issued vs. payments actually received from customers.

Which invoices are truly paid?

i/c Intercompany

Between two entities of the same group. One’s receivable must equal the other’s payable.

Subsidiary A sells to Subsidiary B — both sides must tie.

B/S Balance-sheet / GL

Each account balance vs. the supporting detail behind it. The month-end sweep.

Does every account actually add up to what it claims?

Intercompany is famously the ugliest: different currencies, different accounting calendars, and internal transfer pricing mean “equal and opposite” rarely lines up on the first pass.

§4

How it’s actually done today

The workflow is the same everywhere. How much is manual is what varies — and it’s a lot.

  1. Gather the records

    Pull data from every source: the ERP/accounting system, bank portals, card processors, payment gateways (PSPs), spreadsheets. Often exported by hand as CSVs.

    manual: downloading & formatting files

  2. Normalize & line them up

    Different systems name things differently, use different date formats, currencies, references. Everything has to be forced into a comparable shape first.

    manual: cleaning, VLOOKUPs, copy-paste

  3. Match

    Pair up entries that represent the same transaction. Simple cases match on amount + date + reference. Most transactions match automatically or quickly.

  4. Surface the exceptions

    Whatever doesn’t match is an “exception” — timing differences, duplicates, missing entries, fees, fraud signals, one payment covering many invoices.

  5. Investigate root cause

    A human chases each exception: emails, portal digging, asking another team. This is where the hours go and where “tribal knowledge” lives.

    manual: the real bottleneck

  6. Adjust & document

    Post correcting entries, write notes explaining each difference, attach evidence for the auditor.

    manual: journal entries + narrative

  7. Review & sign off

    A senior reviewer approves; the account is declared reconciled for the period. Repeat next month, forever.

The dirty secret of “automated” recon is that matching is the easy part. Even good software auto-matches the bulk of transactions — but the leftover slice is where all the pain, time and risk concentrate:

Split is illustrative of the widely-cited “automation handles ~80%, exception management is the rest” framing (Numeric, Entries). Actual rates vary by business.

§5

The painpoints — what your friends are fighting

This is the section to internalize. These are the wounds a product would heal.

01

Everything lives in spreadsheets

Excel is still the default reconciliation “tool.” It’s fragile, unversioned, and error-prone at scale.

~90% of spreadsheets contain errors · 50% of teams cite Excel-driven processes as a blocker
02

It eats enormous time

A recurring, unglamorous grind that repeats every single month and swallows the team right when the close deadline hits.

25–40% of finance time · up to ~50 hrs/month on cash recon
03

Data is fragmented & messy

Many banks, PSPs, currencies, ERPs — each with its own format, naming and timing. Just getting the data comparable is half the battle.

missing references, mismatched amounts, multi-currency, multi-entity
04

Exceptions are the real monster

Recon isn’t a matching problem, it’s an exception-management problem. One-to-many and many-to-many matches, timing gaps, duplicates — none fit a simple rule.

the ~20% that rules can’t clear
05

Knowledge lives in people’s heads

“Oh, that difference is always the Singapore entity’s FX timing.” When logic lives in a reviewer’s memory instead of the system, it breaks when they’re on leave — or quit.

tribal knowledge · no audit trail of why
06

It doesn’t scale

Double the transactions, entities or payment providers and the manual effort grows faster than linearly. Cross-team dependencies stall everything.

56% cite cross-team dependencies · 37% cite staff/capacity gaps
07

Error & compliance risk

A missed exception can mean a misstated balance, a failed audit, or undetected fraud. The stakes make the manual fragility genuinely dangerous.

audit findings · restatements · fraud slipping through
§6

The insight that matters for a product

If you remember one thing from this whole report, make it this.

The core wedge

Reconciliation is not a matching problem — it’s an exception-management problem. Matching the easy 80% is close to solved. The money, the misery and the moat are all in the leftover exceptions: investigating why things don’t tie, and capturing the human judgment that resolves them.

Most existing tools are proud of high auto-match rates (vendors advertise 97–99%). But a founder should notice what they’re quietly not solving well:

gap Root-cause investigation

Tools flag an exception; a human still has to figure out why and chase it across systems and teams.

gap Capturing judgment

The reasoning that resolves an exception rarely gets recorded, so the system never learns and the next person starts from scratch.

gap Messy real-world data

Unstructured references, one-payment-for-many-invoices, multi-currency, mid-period rule changes break rigid engines.

gap Setup & cost

Enterprise suites are powerful but slow to implement and priced out of reach for smaller teams (see §7).

§8

Does Tally already do this?

Worth answering head-on — Tally runs a huge share of Indian SMB accounting.

Does TallyPrime automate reconciliation? Partly — the easy half.

Yes, genuinely — and more than people assume. TallyPrime (especially Release 6.0) ships real bank-reconciliation automation: it auto-imports bank statements, and with Connected Banking it fetches statements straight from 145+ Indian banks (SBI, HDFC, ICICI, Axis, Kotak and regionals), shows live balances, auto-reconciles matches, and can auto-create vouchers for missing entries.

But per Tally’s own documentation, it automatically reconciles exact matches — and hands everything else to a human. That line is the whole story.

✓ What Tally automates

  • Auto-import of bank statements (Excel, CSV, MT940)
  • Connected Banking — fetch statements + live balance in-app (Release 6.0)
  • Exact-match auto-reconciliation against your books
  • Suggests potential matches using instrument date, amount and party
  • Auto-create vouchers from bank statement lines
  • Configurable matching rules and multiple recon views

✗ What still lands on a human

  • Reviewing/approving every suggested “potential match”
  • Many-to-one / one-to-many matching — manual selection
  • Partial matches (bank charges, rounding) — you alter or add the entry yourself
  • Unmatched exceptions (only-in-books / only-in-bank) — manual
  • Root-cause investigation — nothing explains why an item is off
  • Scope: it’s bank recon. Not multi-PSP, marketplace, or intercompany at scale
The punchline

Tally automates the ~80% that already matches cleanly and leaves you the ~20% of exceptions — the same split from §4, and precisely the gap identified in §6. So “has Tally already solved this?” → no. It solved the easy half. Its engine is rules-and-suggestions, not agentic: nothing investigates, nothing learns from how you resolved the same exception last month.

Caveat worth checking with your friends: Tally dominates Indian SMB accounting, but larger companies typically run SAP, Oracle or NetSuite — and their recon pain lives in different tools. Confirm which stack they’re actually on before assuming Tally is the context.

§9

What AI & “agentic” solutions others are offering

The space is moving fast and is genuinely crowded. Know it before you build.

The distinction that matters most

AI-assisted = the model suggests, a human approves. Agentic = the loop closes by itself for most cases, and humans only see exceptions above a confidence threshold. Almost every “AI reconciliation” claim you’ll read is the first one wearing the second one’s clothes.

tier 1Accounting incumbents bolting on AITally · Zoho · Xero · QuickBooks

Zoho Books (Feb 2026) added an AI agent for reconciliation: field prediction from past behaviour, bulk “Quick Categorize” (50 txns at once), centralized bank rules across accounts.

Xero’s JAX auto-reconciliation is described as class-leading for high-volume accounts. QuickBooks offers AI Assist for categorization and transaction review. Tally — see §8.

All sit at AI-assisted: better matching and categorization for SMBs, human still owns exceptions.

tier 2Enterprise close suites going agenticBlackLine · HighRadius · Trintech

HighRadius is the loudest: claims 200+ AI agents, 99% accuracy, 100% GL account coverage and 60%+ of close tasks automated. BlackLine runs Verity AI on its Studio360 architecture with advertised 97–99% auto-match.

Powerful, expensive, and aimed at enterprises — the incumbents from §7, now with agents attached.

tier 3Recon specialists — including India-nativeCointab · Osfin · Kosh.ai · Nanonets

This is your actual competitive set. Cointab connects banks, PSPs, ERPs, vendors and marketplaces with reusable workflows and AI-assisted matching. Osfin offers a low-code platform with 170+ connectors and case studies claiming 90–99% automation. Kosh.ai does reconciliation + treasury + payouts in real time. Nanonets does AI transaction matching and discrepancy detection.

These already attack the multi-source, multi-PSP problem Tally doesn’t touch.

tier 4Agentic-native / modern closeNumeric · Kognitos · Trullion

Newer, AI-first tooling built for fast deployment rather than enterprise configuration projects. Vendors claim agentic systems learn from past resolutions and remove up to 80% of manual intervention from the close.

Two market signals worth holding onto:

signal The shift is real

Gartner projects 44% of finance teams will operate with agentic AI components by end of 2026. Agents are claimed to compress close cycles from 10–15 days to 3–5.

signal MCP is opening the door

MCP servers now exist for QuickBooks, Xero and Zoho Books, letting AI agents read accounting data directly — a real integration path. Notably absent: Tally.

⚠︎ Every accuracy/automation percentage in this section is a vendor marketing claim, self-reported and unaudited. Treat them as positioning, not benchmarks — and be suspicious of any “99%” that doesn’t say 99% of what.

§10

What this means for your project

A founder’s reading of the above — questions to take back to your friends.

◆ How to think about the opportunity
  • Don’t sell “matching.” That’s commoditized — Tally, Zoho, Xero and every close suite already do it. Sell exception resolution — the ~20% that costs the hours.
  • Tally is not your competitor — it’s your evidence. It automates exact matches and hands back everything hard (§8). Anyone on Tally still has the painful half unsolved.
  • Pick a wedge type. Bank recon is broadest; intercompany is ugliest (and stickiest). Ask your friends which one bleeds most where they work.
  • Capture the judgment. If your tool records why an exception was resolved and reuses it next month, you build a moat rule engines don’t have. This is the one thing tiers 1–4 all claim and few demonstrably do.
  • Respect the crowd. Cointab, Osfin, Kosh.ai and Nanonets are already here (§9). “We automate reconciliation” is not a differentiator in 2026 — be specific about which exceptions you close autonomously and how you prove it.
  • Watch the segment gap. Enterprise tools are costly and slow; smaller teams are underserved. Where do your friends’ companies actually sit — Tally SMB, or SAP/NetSuite enterprise?
  • Questions to ask them: Which recon takes longest? Which system is the book of record? How many sources/PSPs? What % auto-matches today? Where does an exception physically get investigated — email? Slack? A person’s memory?
§11

Glossary — so no term trips you up

General Ledger (GL)
The company’s master record of every financial transaction — its official “books.”
ERP
The big software system (SAP, NetSuite, Oracle) that runs finance/operations and holds the ledger.
Close / month-end close
The monthly ritual of finalizing the books. Reconciliation is a core step in it.
Exception
Any item that doesn’t match automatically and needs a human to investigate.
PSP / payment gateway
A payment service provider (Stripe, Razorpay, etc.) that processes transactions — another data source to reconcile.
AP / AR
Accounts Payable (money you owe) / Accounts Receivable (money owed to you).
Intercompany
Transactions between entities inside the same corporate group that must net to zero.
Auto-match rate
The % of transactions software pairs up with no human help — the number vendors love to quote.
Timing difference
A gap caused only by when something was recorded vs. cleared — real, but not an error.
SOX / audit
Compliance regimes that effectively mandate reconciled, documented accounts.
Agentic AI
Software that acts on its own and closes the loop, vs. “AI-assisted” which only suggests and waits for a human to approve.
Connected Banking
Tally’s feature that pulls statements and balances directly from your bank into the software, no manual export.
MCP
Model Context Protocol — a standard letting AI agents plug into external systems (now available for QuickBooks, Xero, Zoho Books).
